## Summary

Skipping the wedding planner saves the $5,000 it would cost and creates a second job: roughly 200 decisions across six months. Which photographer earns the price. When invitations go out. Whether a florist's quote is fair or the budget is about to break. Checklists organize the tasks. Judgment on the money stays with two people doing this for the first time.

**Marrymap** generates a full planning timeline from the wedding date and venue, then breaks it into weekly steps. Vendor inquiry emails draft in the couple's own voice. Competing quotes line up side by side, with flags on any item priced outside the typical range. Budget tracking connects to bank data and catches overruns before they compound. One dashboard shows what is done, what comes next, and what sits at risk, so couples approve and adjust while the coordination runs underneath.

Version one covers timeline generation and vendor communication. Email integrations draft and track messages to florists, caterers, and photographers. Couples drop in the quotes that come back, and anything priced past the budget surfaces on its own. First ten couples reveal which decisions spike the sharpest anxiety, and those automate first. Day-of scheduling and seating layout wait until core planning proves out and couples trust the tool with real vendor money.

Pricing runs $29/month for a straightforward wedding and $99 for a large, complex one, against the planner's fee they skipped. Referral is built into the calendar: each wedding sends newly engaged friends in behind it. Venue partnerships and vendor commissions add a second revenue line.

## Competitive Landscape

### Market structure
- **Wedding planning apps (direct proxy):** ~$0.924B (2025) → $1.07B (2026) → $4B by 2035 at a strong **15.5% CAGR**.
- **Broader wedding services/planner market:** ~$300–340B mid-2020s → $500–613B by early-mid 2030s (~6.5–7.7% CAGR).
- **Regional:** North America ~38–42% of app revenue; APAC fastest-growing (~30–34% share). India: $6.42B (FY24) → $16.53B (FY32), 12.55% CAGR.
- **Maturity:** app segment is early-growth with a concentrated top tier (~46% share) and a long fragmented tail (~54%). AI-native full-service planning is very early — incumbents only partially integrate AI.

### Direct competitors
- **The Knot / WeddingWire (The Knot Worldwide).** Leading marketplaces + content + tools. ~73% of wedding-planning AI answers route to just The Knot and Zola. Strengths: brand trust, vendor coverage, SEO, registry. Weaknesses: modular/shallow tools, little automation for outreach/quote comparison. Monetizes vendors, not couples.
- **Zola.** Registry-first, growing planning tools. Strong modern UX among younger couples. Planning tools still mostly checklists/reminders; limited neutral quote evaluation.
- **Aisle Planner.** Pro-grade planning/PM platform for planners. Deep workflow tooling but too complex for DIY couples; a B2B-integration candidate rather than a direct consumer rival.
- **Generic AI assistants (ChatGPT, Gemini).** Flexible and cheap, but no structured wedding data, no vendor/budget integration, no persistent workspace.

### Indirect competitors & workarounds
Human planners ($5–15K, limited supply) · all-inclusive venues · static checklists/spreadsheets · general PM tools (Notion, Trello) · "spreadsheet + ChatGPT" combos · Facebook/Reddit groups as crowdsourced planners.

### Market gaps (where this idea fits)
- **Mid-budget couples skipping planners** ($15–40K weddings) overwhelmed by 200+ decisions — helped with discovery but not execution.
- **"Planner-like value at $20–100/mo"** tier between free checklists and $5–15K planners.
- **Vendor outreach automation, quote normalization/comparison, budget-risk management, end-to-end orchestration.**
- **International/APAC and non-English markets** underserved by localized, culturally-aware AI tools.

### Competitive advantages to build
Vertical AI orchestration fine-tuned on wedding timelines/quotes · workflow integrations (email, calendar, doc parsing) raising switching costs · two-sided learning network from vendor response data · high-resolution "wedding orchestration dataset" · sharp brand positioning as the "AI planner that replaces a $5–15K human."

### Key risks
Rapid AI catch-up by incumbents (could bundle AI into free tools) · consumer trust in AI for high-stakes planning · monetization pressure where many tools are free · day-of coordination still needs humans · cultural/regulatory nuance across regions.

**Bottom line:** the strongest opportunity is a **vertical, end-to-end orchestrator SaaS** — automating vendor outreach and quote evaluation, managing timelines/tasks/budgets dynamically, targeting DIY and mid-budget couples globally (starting NA + India) — moving before incumbents fully pivot.
