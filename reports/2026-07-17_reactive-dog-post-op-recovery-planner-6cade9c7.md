## Summary

Vet surgical practices send dogs home after knee and joint operations with generic recovery sheets. Those sheets assume calm animals, open floor plans, and owners who can lift 90 pounds. Reality: high-energy breeds in small apartments, nervous dogs refusing crate rest, elderly owners managing alone. Callbacks spike. The protocol works inside the clinic; it falls apart at home.

**Pawscript** builds adaptive post-op plans for each case before the patient leaves the building. A vet tech fills out a 5-minute intake covering the animal's breed, behavior profile (crate-refusal, separation anxiety), living space, other pets, stairs, and the caretaker's physical limits. From that intake, the system generates a staged recovery plan with confinement setups sized for the apartment, enrichment routines for high-drive breeds, harness recommendations for caretakers who struggle to lift, and escalation scripts for late-night emergencies.

Ship to 3 pilot surgical clinics doing large volumes of knee and joint procedures. Build the MVP with a Typeform-style questionnaire feeding a rules engine and template library. Integrate with Cornerstone or ezyVet so the plan lives inside existing workflows. Track each clinic's 30-day callback rate against baseline and refine the sections that move it most. Growth comes from veterinary surgery conferences, partnerships with rehab brands like Ruffwear and Walkin' Pets, and word of mouth in specialist referral networks. Pricing runs $199–$499 per practice per month, tiered by procedure volume, with a $29 direct-to-caretaker app and insurer compliance data as second revenue layers.

## The Idea in Detail

During dog surgeries like TPLO, recovery is stressful for owners — especially with high-energy or anxious dogs. Generic instructions ignore unique home environments and pet dynamics, leading to chaos and noncompliance. The app gathers data on the dog's behavior, home setup, and owner capacity to craft a tailored recovery and enrichment plan, helping clinics issue clear, customized instructions that stay aligned with medical advice while fitting a realistic home environment.

**Pain points addressed:** generic post-op instructions fail with high-energy, large, or multi-dog households, causing stress, noncompliance, and recovery delays; owners are overwhelmed by managing restrictions and enrichment, driving extra clinic calls.

**Target audience:** veterinary clinics performing orthopedic procedures (TPLO/CCL); pet owners managing complex post-surgery care.

**Key challenges:** integration with existing clinic systems may be complex and resisted by traditional practices; personalization must be accurate and comprehensive to ensure compliance.

**Strategic moats:** partnerships with leading vet clinics for credibility and market insight; proprietary personalization tuned to individual pet scenarios and outcome data.

**Related angles:** a version for cats or other pets; a platform integrating real-time owner updates to adjust plans dynamically.

## Direct Competitors

There is no obvious, scaled "reactive‑dog post‑op planner" yet; competition comes from broader vet SaaS and generic pet‑health apps. Below are the most relevant categories and representative competitors.

### 1. Vet client communication & workflow platforms

**a. PetDesk / similar reminder platforms** (category representative)

- **Background:** PetDesk, VitusVet, etc., provide reminders, 2‑way SMS, loyalty, and simple patient education tools for clinics.
- **Market size/share:** Market is fragmented; these players hold material share of US small‑animal clinics but data is not public.
- **Core offerings:** Automated appointment & vaccine reminders; mass messaging; client app for basic records and communication.
- **Strengths:** Deep installed base; integrated with PIMS; clinic workflows well understood.
- **Weaknesses:** Education is templated and generic, not behavior‑aware; no procedure‑specific, week‑by‑week recovery engines.
- **Pricing:** Monthly SaaS per clinic (often USD 200–500+ depending on modules).

**b. Tele‑vet platforms (e.g., TeleVet, Airvet, Vetster – category)**

- **Core offerings:** On‑demand or scheduled virtual consults, chat, and follow‑up care.
- **Strengths:** Access to vets between visits; triage of "is this normal?" post‑op questions.
- **Weaknesses:** No structured, long‑horizon recovery planning; reactive behavior management is ad‑hoc per consult.
- **UVP vs. you:** Human‑in‑the‑loop advice rather than algorithmic plans. Your planner could integrate with them.

### 2. Condition‑specific digital rehab tools

Small apps/courses for canine physio and TPLO recovery (often single‑practitioner brands) are typically static video libraries or PDFs, owner‑purchased rather than clinic‑integrated, and focused on exercises rather than full behavior + environment + clinic protocol integration. They compete for the owner's attention but weakly on clinic workflow and personalization.

### 3. Generic pet‑health & training apps

- **Pupford, Dogo, GoodPup, etc.:** training/behavior apps that sometimes cover crate rest, reactivity, and enrichment.
- **Strengths:** Mature B2C UX, large user bases, lots of content about reactivity and enrichment.
- **Weaknesses:** Not surgery‑aware; do not embed medical restrictions; no clinic connection or discharge workflow support.

- **Facebook & Reddit communities:** Large TPLO recovery groups and subreddits act as crowdsourced planners; very high engagement but low reliability and not clinic‑aligned.

- **Geographic:** North America easiest initial market; Western Europe, Australia/NZ under‑served by English‑language digital tools; urban APAC fast‑growing with a localization gap.
- **Price points:** no low‑friction, low‑cost product for clinics not ready for full integration (e.g., QR‑coded owner plans at USD 50–100/mo per hospital); no distinct premium owner tier for deep behavioral support.

## Competitive Advantages Assessment

- **Technology barriers:** rules‑based personalized planning isn't hard; the barrier is clinical nuance + workflow fit. Over time, aggregated outcome data becomes a harder‑to‑copy engine.
- **Network effects:** weak direct effects; but data network effects at the protocol level ("our TPLO plan is validated on 5,000 dogs").
- **Scale benefits:** content production and PIMS/tele‑vet integrations yield increasing returns.
- **Data advantages:** structured adherence, behavior scores, and complication flags underpin clinical studies and an "evidence‑backed discharge" B2B story.
- **Brand value:** a brand focused on "behavior‑aware orthopedic recovery" can own a distinctive niche; anchor it in depth and outcomes, not just "we have an app."
- **Regulatory moats:** minimal — not a drug or device; some moat from compliance posture (data security, VCPR boundaries).

## Entry Barriers

- **Capital:** moderate; MVP buildable with a small team, scaling needs more but far below hardware/pharma.
- **Technology:** rules engine, owner app, clinic dashboard, PIMS integrations, secure storage — high reliability and UX raise the bar for copycats.

- **Switching costs:** meaningful for clinics after 1–2 years (templates, staff training); minimal for owners (time‑bound episode).
- **IP:** algorithms/UX unlikely to be strongly patentable; IP resides in content libraries, data, and integrations.

## Critical Assessment

- **Assumption — post‑op guidance is a top clinic priority:** many owners rank revenue‑driving tools higher; you must prove hard ROI (fewer nurse calls, fewer revisits, higher satisfaction).
- **Assumption — reactive/anxious positioning is the wedge:** distinctive and painful, but some clinics may see it as too niche; may need to market as "for all dogs, with special strength in difficult ones."
- **Assumption — clinics will fill out intake:** time‑pressed staff may skip it; owner self‑intake via mobile may be needed.
- **Blind spots:** nurse/tech adoption friction, liability concerns around content‑driven suggestions, and owner digital literacy/overload.

## Opportunity Spaces

- **Underserved segments:** high‑complexity ortho + reactive dogs (launch wedge); corporate specialty groups; rehab‑light regions.
- **Feature gaps:** pre‑discharge intake; staged visual plans; owner‑clinic feedback loop; behavior modules (leash reactivity, separation distress, crate aversion) within surgical limits.
- **Geographic:** start North America specialty ortho/referral centers, then UK/EU and Australia.
- **Partnerships:** rehab/physio chains (white‑label), tele‑vet platforms, insurers, and harness/sling/recovery‑suit brands.
- **Technology leverage:** rules engine + outcome data; owner‑side UX innovation; lightweight interoperability first (webhooks, QR links), then deeper PIMS APIs.

## Summary

The landscape is characterized by rapidly growing adjacent spend (rehab, wound care, pain) but a glaring lack of specialized software that converts clinic protocols into behavior‑ and home‑aware recovery journeys. Direct competition comes mainly from generic vet communication SaaS and training apps, none of which deeply address the intersection of orthopedic surgery, reactive/anxious dogs, and constrained home environments. The most promising path is to own the "difficult dog" orthopedic recovery niche within specialty and high‑volume surgical clinics, then expand horizontally. Critical risks are clinic adoption inertia, perception as a nice‑to‑have, and fast‑follower moves by PIMS or tele‑vet vendors — mitigated by ruthless workflow fit, co‑developed protocols, and early outcome evidence.
